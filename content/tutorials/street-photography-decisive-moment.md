## The Decisive Moment

Henri Cartier-Bresson coined the phrase *"the decisive moment"* — the instant when form and content align perfectly, when a photograph achieves both geometric harmony and emotional truth simultaneously. It's a concept that sounds abstract but becomes visceral when you're on the street with a camera.

## Equipment Philosophy

**Use the smallest camera you can.** The best street photographers — from Vivian Maier to Daido Moriyama — chose compact, inconspicuous tools. A Leica M, a Fujifilm X100, a Sony RX100, or even a capable smartphone will serve you better than a DSLR with a telephoto lens that announces your presence from across the street.

**Prime lenses are your friend.** A 35mm or 50mm equivalent focal length forces you to engage with your subject at a human distance. Zoom lenses encourage lazy composition and physical distance from the scene.

## The Mental Game

Street photography is 80% psychology and 20% technique.

### Overcoming the Fear

Most new street photographers are paralysed by fear of confrontation. Here's the truth: **the vast majority of people don't notice you, and those who do rarely care.** Know your local laws around photography in public spaces. In most countries, photographing people in public places is entirely legal.

Practice with a strategy:

1. **The tourist approach** — act like you're photographing the buildings and let people walk through
2. **The zone focusing method** — pre-focus at a set distance (e.g., 3 metres), shoot from the hip
3. **Direct engagement** — ask permission, get a portrait, learn their story

## Technical Preparation

### Zone Focusing

Set your aperture to f/8 or f/11 and focus manually to approximately 3 metres. At these settings, most things from 2–5 metres will be acceptably sharp. This eliminates autofocus lag — you can shoot the instant you see the moment.

### Camera Settings for Street

- **Shutter speed:** Minimum 1/250s to freeze motion
- **ISO:** Shoot in Auto-ISO with a minimum shutter speed set
- **RAW format** for maximum flexibility in post

## Compositional Thinking

Look for **layers** — foreground, midground, background elements that create depth. Find **frames within frames** — doorways, windows, arches. Seek **geometric contrast** — a curved subject against straight lines, or vice versa.

Most importantly: **get closer.** Robert Capa's famous advice applies universally. Most beginner street photographers shoot from too far away.

## Building a Body of Work

Great street photography is about sustained, committed practice in a specific place over time. Choose your neighbourhood, your city centre, your market. Return again and again. Develop a relationship with the place and the people in it. This is how Vivian Maier created her extraordinary archive of Chicago street life — not through grand expeditions, but through daily, devoted attention to the world immediately around her.

Shoot 100 frames to get 10 worth keeping. Shoot 1,000 frames to find 1 truly exceptional image. This is the discipline.
