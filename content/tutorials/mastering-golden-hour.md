## What Is Golden Hour?

Golden hour refers to the period shortly after sunrise and shortly before sunset, when the sun is low on the horizon and produces warm, directional light with long, soft shadows. The light during this time is fundamentally different from midday light — it's diffused, rich in warm wavelengths, and creates a three-dimensional quality in images that is almost impossible to replicate.

## Planning Your Shot

The most important tool for golden hour photography isn't your camera — it's an app like **PhotoPills** or **The Photographer's Ephemeris**. These apps allow you to:

- Calculate the exact time of sunrise and sunset for any location on any date
- See where the sun will rise and set on the horizon
- Plan compositions months in advance using augmented reality

### Key Planning Steps

1. **Scout your location** at least 24 hours in advance, ideally at the same time of day
2. **Arrive early** — at least 30 minutes before sunrise, or 45 minutes before sunset
3. **Note your key compositions** so you're not searching when the light arrives
4. **Check the weather** — clouds are your friends during golden hour

## Camera Settings

Golden hour light changes rapidly. Here's a starting framework:

| Setting | Value |
|---------|-------|
| ISO | 100–400 |
| Aperture | f/8–f/16 for landscapes |
| White Balance | Daylight or Cloudy (warmer) |
| Mode | Manual or Aperture Priority |

### The Exposure Triangle at Golden Hour

Because the light is soft and directional, you can achieve beautiful results at lower ISOs than you might expect. The key is to **expose for the highlights** to prevent blown-out skies, then recover shadow detail in post-processing.

## Composition Tips

- **Include foreground interest** — the directional light will add texture and depth to grass, sand, or rocks
- **Look for long shadows** — they create leading lines and a sense of scale
- **Shoot into the light** — backlit subjects during golden hour create stunning rim lighting and silhouettes
- **Use a polarising filter** to manage reflections and deepen blues in the sky

## Post-Processing

Minimal processing is often best for golden hour images. In Lightroom:

1. Pull back **highlights** to recover cloud detail
2. Open up **shadows** slightly
3. Increase **clarity** modestly to enhance texture
4. Warm the **white balance** slightly if needed
5. Enhance **orange and yellow** tones in the HSL panel

## Your Assignment

For the next two weekends, commit to shooting golden hour at least once. Choose a location you know well — familiarity with the place lets you focus entirely on the light. Share your results in the club forum with a brief note on your planning process.
