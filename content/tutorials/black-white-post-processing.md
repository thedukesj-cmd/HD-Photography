## Seeing in Black and White

Before you convert a single image, you need to train yourself to *see* in black and white. This means evaluating a scene not by its colours, but by its **tonal values**, **textures**, **shapes**, and **contrast**.

The classic red poppy in a green field looks spectacular in colour. In black and white, the red and green translate to nearly identical mid-tones — and the image collapses. Conversely, a face with strong side lighting that looks ordinary in colour becomes dramatic and sculptural in black and white.

## Not All Conversions Are Equal

The worst way to create a black and white image is to simply desaturate it. This treats all colours equally and produces flat, lifeless results.

### The Luminosity Method

In Lightroom, use the **Black & White Mix** panel (in HSL/Color). Each slider controls how a specific colour in the original image is rendered as a tone in the conversion:

- **Drag the Red slider up** to brighten skin tones and add drama to red subjects
- **Drag the Blue slider down** to darken skies and make clouds pop
- **Drag the Green slider down** to darken foliage and add contrast to landscapes
- **Drag the Orange slider up** to brighten warm skin tones further

### Developing a Signature Look

Spend time developing your personal black and white style. Study photographers whose monochrome work you admire:

- **Ansel Adams**: Zone System, extraordinary tonal range, technical perfection
- **Sebastião Salgado**: Deep, rich blacks, cinematic contrast, humanitarian gravitas
- **Daido Moriyama**: Grain-heavy, high contrast, raw and gritty
- **Sally Mann**: Painterly, soft, often blurred — emotional rather than technical

## Film Emulation

Film grain is not noise. Properly rendered grain adds organic texture and reinforces the emotional register of a black and white image. In Lightroom, add grain conservatively (Amount: 20–35, Size: 25–40, Roughness: 50). Alternatively, use dedicated presets from RNI Films or VSCO that simulate classic emulsions like Kodak Tri-X or Ilford HP5.

## Final Checklist for B&W Processing

- [ ] Evaluate tonal contrast — do the key elements separate from each other?
- [ ] Check the deepest shadows — are there rich, detailed blacks?
- [ ] Check the brightest highlights — detail preserved or intentionally blown?
- [ ] Add selective grain to taste
- [ ] Apply a subtle vignette to draw the eye inward
- [ ] Convert to sRGB before exporting for web or print
