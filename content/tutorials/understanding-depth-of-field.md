## What Controls Depth of Field?

Three factors determine how much of your image appears sharp:

1. **Aperture** — The most controllable factor. Wider apertures (lower f-numbers like f/1.8) produce shallower DOF; narrower apertures (higher f-numbers like f/16) produce deeper DOF.
2. **Focal length** — Longer focal lengths (telephoto) compress depth of field at any given aperture.
3. **Subject distance** — The closer you are to your subject, the shallower the depth of field.

## Creative Applications

### Portraits: Isolation and Intimacy

For portraiture, a shallow depth of field — typically f/1.4 to f/2.8 — separates the subject from the background beautifully. The out-of-focus background (bokeh) draws the viewer's eye directly to the face.

The key is **accurate focus on the eyes**, specifically the eye closest to the camera. At f/1.4, the depth of field can be razor-thin — a few millimetres at close portrait distances.

### Landscapes: Front-to-Back Sharpness

For landscape photography, you often want everything sharp from the closest foreground element to the distant horizon. Shoot at f/11–f/16 and use **hyperfocal distance** focusing — focus at the hyperfocal distance for your lens/aperture combination to maximise depth of field.

### Macro: The Challenge of Extreme Close-Up

At 1:1 macro distances, depth of field collapses to fractions of a millimetre even at f/16. Many macro photographers use **focus stacking** — taking multiple images at different focus points and blending them in post-processing software.

## Practical Exercise

Find a subject with depth — a row of flowers, a line of books, a garden path. Photograph it at f/1.8, f/5.6, f/11, and f/16. Compare the results. Notice how depth of field interacts with your compositional choices and the visual story each image tells.
